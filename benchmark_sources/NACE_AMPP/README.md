# NACE/AMPP (Association for Materials Protection and Performance) Benchmark Test Sources

## Overview
NACE International (now AMPP - Association for Materials Protection and Performance) is the leading authority on corrosion control and materials protection for pipelines. Their certifications are essential for corrosion engineers and cathodic protection specialists.

## Recommended Test Categories for NACE/AMPP

### Category 1: Cathodic Protection Fundamentals
- Electrochemical corrosion principles
- CP system types (galvanic vs. impressed current)
- CP criteria (-850 mV, 100 mV polarization)
- Measurement techniques

### Category 2: CP Design
- Current requirement calculations
- Groundbed design
- Anode selection and sizing
- Rectifier sizing
- Attenuation calculations

### Category 3: CP Testing and Troubleshooting
- Structure-to-electrolyte potential measurements
- IR drop and correction methods
- Current density measurements
- Interference testing
- Short circuit investigation

### Category 4: Pipeline Coatings
- Coating types and selection
- Holiday detection
- Coating damage assessment
- Shielding effects

### Category 5: Internal Corrosion
- Corrosion mechanisms (CO2, H2S, O2)
- Corrosion inhibitors
- Internal corrosion monitoring
- Corrosion rate calculations

## Sample Test Questions (To Be Developed)

### Question 1: CP Criteria Application
**Scenario**: A buried steel pipeline has the following potential measurements:
- Native potential: -650 mV vs. Cu/CuSO4
- Polarized potential: -820 mV vs. Cu/CuSO4
- IR drop: 50 mV

**Task**: Determine if the pipeline meets NACE SP0169 criteria for cathodic protection and explain your reasoning.

**Expected Elements**:
- Application of -850 mV criterion
- Application of 100 mV polarization criterion
- IR drop correction
- Conclusion on protection status

**Standards**: NACE SP0169 (Control of External Corrosion on Underground or Submerged Metallic Piping Systems)

---

### Question 2: CP Current Requirements
**Scenario**: A 10-mile coated pipeline requires cathodic protection. The pipeline specifications are:
- Diameter: 20 inches
- Coating: Fusion-bonded epoxy (FBE)
- Soil resistivity: 5,000 ohm-cm
- Desired protection level: -950 mV

**Task**: Estimate the total current required for the entire pipeline length.

**Expected Elements**:
- Current density estimation (typically 1-5 mA/m² for coated pipelines)
- Surface area calculation
- Current requirement calculation
- Safety factor application

**Standards**: NACE SP0169, Peabody's Control of Pipeline Corrosion

---

### Question 3: IR Drop Measurement and Correction
**Scenario**: A pipeline potential measurement shows -780 mV vs. Cu/CuSO4. An instant-off potential measurement shows -830 mV vs. Cu/CuSO4.

**Task**: Calculate the IR drop and determine if the pipeline meets NACE criteria.

**Expected Elements**:
- IR drop calculation (on-potential minus instant-off)
- True polarized potential determination
- Criterion compliance assessment
- Explanation of instant-off measurement technique

**Standards**: NACE SP0169, Section 6

---

### Question 4: Internal Corrosion Assessment
**Scenario**: A sour gas pipeline (H2S: 500 ppm, CO2: 2%) operates at 800 psig and 120°F. The pipeline is 25 years old with wall thickness 0.375 inches. Previous inspection showed 15% wall loss.

**Task**: Estimate the corrosion rate, calculate remaining life, and recommend inspection frequency.

**Expected Elements**:
- Corrosion rate estimation (considering H2S and CO2 effects)
- Remaining life calculation
- Inspection interval recommendation
- Mitigation strategies (inhibitors, corrosion-resistant alloys)

**Standards**: NACE MR0175/ISO 15156, API 1160

## Notes

- NACE merged with SSPC to form AMPP in 2021
- CP certifications are tiered (CP 1 → CP 2 → CP 3 → CP 4)
- CP 4 requires 6-12 years of experience
- NACE SP0169 is the primary standard for external corrosion control
- NACE MR0175/ISO 15156 covers materials for sour service
- Peabody's Control of Pipeline Corrosion is the definitive textbook
- Many questions involve calculations (Ohm's Law, Faraday's Law, attenuation)
